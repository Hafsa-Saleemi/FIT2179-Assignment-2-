Coral Bleaching
a degrading marine ecosystem

The Great Barrier Reef has survived cycles of damage and recovery. The danger now is that heat stress, bleaching and other disturbances are arriving faster than the reef can rebuild.

By Hafsa Saleemi

Coral reefs are among the most diverse and valuable ecosystems on Earth, supporting marine biodiversity, coastal protection, tourism, and local livelihoods. The Great Barrier Reef (GBR), the world's largest coral reef system, has experienced repeated disturbances over recent decades, including marine heatwaves, mass bleaching events, cyclones, flooding, and crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks.

This visualisation explores how these pressures have affected the GBR over time. Using data from scientific monitoring programs, it examines the distribution of reefs, the importance of the ecosystem, the increasing intensity of thermal stress, and the resulting changes in coral cover. Together, these visualisations tell the story of a reef under growing pressure and the challenges it faces in recovering from increasingly frequent disturbances.

01

Global Reef Threats: Reef Decline is not isolated

Coral reefs are an important part of the marine ecosystem. They support marine biodiversity, coastal protection, and local economies around the world. However due to climate change, rising ocean temperatures, pollution, and overfishing amongst other things have placed many reefs under threat. This map highlights the global distribution of major coral reefs and the proportion of them considered at risk. While reef decline is a worldwide challenge, we will be zooming into the Great Barrier Reef as it is one of the most important ecosystems in Australia.

02

Inside Australia: The Great Barrier Reef

Stretching over 2,300 kilometres along Australia's north-east coast, the Great Barrier Reef is the largest coral reef system in the world. It consists of thousands of individual reefs distributed across northern, central, and southern regions of Queensland. Conditions vary considerably between locations, which means reef health can differ from one area to another. Understanding this geographic diversity is important when assessing how different parts of the reef respond to environmental stress. This graph shows the current coral cover of each reef, and shows us which regions are most impacted by global warming and so on.

03

Economic Importance: A Reef that supports the country and its people

The Great Barrier Reef is not only an ecological treasure but also a major economic hub. Tourism generates most of its economic value, supporting many businesses and employment across Queensland. Fisheries and recreational activities also contribute to local communities. These benefits mean that reef decline has consequences that extend beyond the environment. Even if one does not care about the environment this issue will directly affect them. The economic impact will be felt not just in areas confined to the reef but at a wider scale.

04

Biodiversity: A living System, not just coral

Despite its name coral reefs do not only consist of coral. It supports an extremely diverse marine ecosystem. The reef supports an extraordinary variety of life, including corals, fish, turtles, sharks, molluscs, and marine mammals. This biodiversity underpins the ecological functioning of the entire ecosystem. Many species depend directly on healthy coral habitats for food, shelter, and reproduction. Protecting coral reefs therefore means protecting thousands of interconnected species that rely on them.

The story changes when the reef moves from disturbance and recovery to disturbance without enough time to recover.

05

Multiple Threats: Many Pressures Act at Once

Coral reefs face a range of pressures including mass bleaching, crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks, cyclones, flooding, and disease. While each threat contributes to reef decline, recent monitoring shows that large-scale bleaching events have become the most significant driver of coral mortality. As such we will explore the events that have caused this increase in coral bleaching.

06

Heat Stress and Bleaching

Coral bleaching occurs when prolonged heat stress disrupts the relationship between corals and the algae that provide them with energy. As ocean temperatures rise, bleaching events become much more frequent and much more severe. This chart demonstrates the strong relationship between increasing heat stress and the occurrence of mass bleaching events across the Great Barrier Reef. As the temperature fluctuates it can be seen that the bleaching events follow closely behind.

The Mean Bleaching Alert is a 0-4 scale measuring how severely heat stress threatens coral, where 4 = mass mortality expected. SST Anomaly is how many degrees warmer the ocean is compared to its historical average for that time of year.

Heat stress during the 2024 event
Degree Heating Weeks (DHW) at peak of 2024 mass bleaching event. Bleaching threshold: 4 DHW. Mortality threshold: 8 DHW.
Large parts of the reef were pushed beyond bleaching and even mortality thresholds during the 2024 event. From: NOAA CoralTemp v3.1, 2024-03-15

Spatial Heat Stress

Heat stress is not distributed evenly across the reef. Some regions experience much higher levels of thermal stress than others. The map highlights where marine heatwaves were most intense during the most recent bleaching event. These patterns help explain why some areas have experienced more severe coral mortality than others.

08

Long-Term Coral Cover Trends: The reef has changed over decades

Coral cover is often used as an indicator of reef health, hard coral means dead coral. Over the past several decades, coral cover has fluctuated through cycles of disturbances from bleaching events, cyclones and outbreaks of crown-of-thorns starfish, and recoveries. Although periods of recovery have occurred, recently there has been an unfortunate increase in poor reef health. As the graphs show, the amount of hard coral has only increased.

12

Recovery Windows Are Shrinking

Historically, reefs often had long periods between major disturbances. These intervals allowed coral reefs to recover before the next major event occurred. In recent decades, bleaching events have become more frequent, reducing the available time for recovery. As recovery windows shrink, the resilience of the reef becomes increasingly weaker until one day they will collapse.

13

Disturbances are becoming more frequent

Major disturbances are occurring more frequently and often overlap with one another. Bleaching events, cyclones, and crown-of-thorns starfish outbreaks continue to place pressure on reef ecosystems. The increasing frequency of these disturbances helps explain why recovery has become more difficult and why the future resilience of the Great Barrier Reef remains uncertain.

The Great Barrier Reef remains is an important ecosystem, but the visualisations reveals a clear pattern of increasing pressure. While reefs have historically demonstrated an ability to recover from disturbance, repeated bleaching events, rising ocean temperatures, and other environmental stressors are reducing the time available for recovery.

The data show that coral decline is not driven by a single event but by the cumulative impact of multiple disturbances occurring with increasing frequency. Although substantial coral cover remains across many parts of the reef, future resilience will depend on whether recovery can keep pace with the growing intensity and frequency of these pressures. Understanding these patterns is essential for protecting one of the Australia's most important marine ecosystems.
